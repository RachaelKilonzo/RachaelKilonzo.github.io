Beyond FICO: Building an AI-Driven Credit Risk Engine

A FinTech Case Study using XGBoost & Python

Project Type: Credit Risk Modeling / FinTech
Tech Stack: Python, Scikit-Learn, XGBoost, Pandas
Performance: 0.74 ROC-AUC on 2.2 Million Records

The Billion-Dollar Balancing Act

Financial institutions face a perpetual dilemma: how do you maximize loan growth without opening the floodgates to defaults? Traditional underwriting relies heavily on static credit scores (like FICO). While effective, this often creates a "credit invisible" gap—rejecting applicants who are financially stable but lack a long history, while approving "prime" borrowers who are actually over-leveraged.

For this project, I set out to build a Probability of Default (PoD) engine. Using the massive Lending Club dataset (2007–2018), I analyzed 2.2 million loan applications representing $34 billion in value to build a machine learning framework capable of distinguishing viable borrowers from high-risk applicants.

1. The Strategy: Preventing Leakage

One of the biggest pitfalls in credit risk modeling is Data Leakage—accidentally using future information (like the current status of a loan) to predict the past. To build a realistic production-ready framework, I adhered to a strict preprocessing strategy:

  • Temporal Filtering: I rigorously removed features that wouldn't exist at the moment of application (e.g., "Total Payment Received"). The model can only see what an underwriter sees.
  • Target Mapping: The dataset is highly imbalanced (11.9% default rate). I mapped 'Charged Off' and 'Default' loans to 1, and 'Fully Paid' to 0.
  • Split-First Engineering: Statistical transformations (like scaling) were fit only on the training set to prevent test-set distribution leakage.

2. Feature Engineering: The "Secret Sauce"

Raw data rarely tells the whole story. I engineered domain-specific features to capture the two pillars of creditworthiness: Intent and Capacity.

Payment-to-Income (PTI) Ratio:

The Logic: A borrower might have a high income, but if their loan installment is huge, they will default.
The Math: (Monthly Installment / (Annual Income / 12))

Credit Utilization Efficiency:

The Logic: This detects borrowers who are relying on revolving credit for liquidity—a major distress signal.

Employment Stability Flag:

The Logic: A Boolean flag for applicants employed for 2+ years, acting as a proxy for income continuity.

Delinquency History:

The Logic: A composite flag combining tax liens, past bankruptcies, and recent delinquencies.

3. What the Data Revealed (EDA)

Before modeling, I visualized the behavioral differences between good and bad borrowers. The engineered features proved their worth immediately.

  • The Affordability Gap: Defaulters had a median PTI ratio of 8.4%, significantly higher than the 7.0% of compliant borrowers.
  • The "History Repeats" Rule: Applicants with any history of delinquency had a default rate of 32.9%—nearly triple the baseline average.
  • Utilization: Defaulters were using 55.5% of their available credit limits on average.

4. Model Selection & Performance

I established a baseline using a Random Forest classifier, but ultimately chose XGBoost for the production model due to its superior handling of class imbalance and gradient boosting capabilities.

The Results

  • ROC-AUC Score: 0.744 (Outperforming Random Forest at 0.718).
  • Precision (Non-Default): 0.94. When the model says a borrower is safe, it is right 94% of the time.
  • Recall (Default): 0.70. The model successfully flagged 70% of the actual defaults in the holdout set.

5. The "Black Box" Explained

For a model to be adopted by a Credit Committee, it must be interpretable. Using XGBoost's gain metrics, I ranked the features that drive the decision-making process.

  • Credit Grade (Sub-Grade): The strongest predictor, acting as an aggregate of bureau health.
  • Interest Rate: Higher rates correlate directly with risk (confirming risk-based pricing logic).
  • Recent Inquiries: The inq_last_6mths feature was a top driver. High velocity of credit-seeking is a massive distress signal.
  • Payment-to-Income (PTI): My engineered feature ranked in the top 5, validating the hypothesis that affordability is just as important as credit history.

6. Business Impact & Next Steps

This model provides more than just a prediction; it offers a framework for automated decision-making. Based on the analysis, I propose three strategic implementations for the lending team:

  1. The 15% Hard Cap: Threshold analysis suggests strictly capping loan approvals at a 15% Payment-to-Income ratio. This single rule would significantly reduce exposure to default-prone loans.
  2. Dynamic Pricing: Instead of broad buckets, use the model's predicted Probability of Default (PoD) to set interest rates at a granular individual level.
  3. The Watch List: Automate a "Manual Review" trigger for any applicant with >1 recent inquiry and >50% credit utilization, regardless of their income level.
