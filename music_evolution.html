The Evolution of the Hallyu Sound: A Generational Data Analysis

Mining the Spotify API to Decode 4 Generations of K-Pop using Python & R

Project Type: Music Data Science / Exploratory Data Analysis
Tech Stack: Python (Spotipy), R (Circlize), Pandas, Seaborn
Dataset: 13,000+ Unique Tracks across 4 Musical Generations

The Sonic DNA of K-Pop

K-Pop has evolved from a local South Korean phenomenon into a dominant global force. But beyond the high-budget music videos and synchronized choreography, the actual *audio architecture* of the music has undergone a radical transformation. From the 90s hip-hop influence of the 1st Generation to the experimental, high-tempo sounds of the 4th Generation, the "vibe" of K-Pop is quantifiable.

In this project, I built an end-to-end pipeline to analyze the evolution of K-Pop. I extracted audio features for over 13,000 tracks via the Spotify Web API, performed rigorous data cleaning in Python, and utilized R to create advanced circular visualizations that map the interconnectedness of musical attributes.

1. Data Extraction: Mining the Spotify API

Using the Spotipy library, I developed a script to crawl through artists categorized by their debut era. This wasn't a simple "search and download" task; it required a strategic approach to ensure data integrity:

  • Artist URI Management: To avoid ambiguity (e.g., artists with similar names), I utilized specific Spotify URIs for legendary groups like H.O.T (1st Gen) through to modern icons like Stray Kids (4th Gen).
  • The Repetition Problem: K-Pop artists frequently release "Repackaged" albums. I implemented a filter to exclude the appears_on album group and prioritized original Korean releases.
  • Feature Mapping: I captured 14 unique audio metrics for every song, including Danceability, Energy, Valence (mood), and Speechiness.

2. Filtering for "Sonic Purity"

Raw music data is noisy. To ensure the analysis reflected the actual creative evolution of the genre, I performed several layers of cleaning:

  • Language Filtering: I removed Japanese and Chinese versions of Korean tracks to prevent duplicate feature weighting for the same composition.
  • Non-Musical Track Removal: I used regex and string matching to drop instrumentals, remixes, and "tour" versions that often skew liveness and loudness metrics.
  • Imbalance Handling: Standardized all song and album names to lowercase to detect and drop duplicates across different regional releases.

3. What the Data Revealed: The Generational Shift

The exploratory analysis provided a clear narrative of how K-Pop has changed to meet modern streaming demands.

  • The Speed of Sound: The 4th Generation has the highest average tempo, reflecting the high-energy, performance-heavy nature of modern hits.
  • The Shrinking Song: Much like the global industry, K-Pop song durations have steadily decreased with each generation to optimize for streaming replayability.
  • Major vs. Minor: K-Pop is overwhelmingly "happy" in its structural mode, with nearly 60% of songs written in a Major key, though this gap narrowed in the 3rd and 4th Generations as "darker" concepts became popular.

4. Visualizing Correlation: The R Chord Diagram

While Python is excellent for data manipulation, I turned to **R** and the chorddiag library to visualize how musical features correlate with one another. Unlike a standard heatmap, a Chord Diagram allows us to see the "flow" of relationship strengths.

The diagram revealed that Energy and Loudness share the strongest link, while Acousticness sits as the outlier, almost always negatively correlated with the high-octane production styles of the modern era. This visualization provides a "fingerprint" of the K-Pop production style.

5. The Mood Scale: Happiest & Saddest Tracks

By sorting by Valence, I identified the sonic extremes of the dataset. Interestingly, the "happiest" songs (high valence) were predominantly from the 1st and 2nd Generations, while the 4th Generation showed a higher density of "intense" but lower-valence tracks, suggesting a move away from traditional "bubblegum pop" toward more complex emotional palettes.

6. Conclusion & Future Directions

This analysis proves that K-Pop's evolution isn't just aesthetic—it is structural. Modern K-Pop is faster, shorter, and more "loud" than its predecessors, optimized perfectly for the viral, dance-heavy era of social media. Future iterations of this project could involve:

  1. Lyric Sentiment Analysis: Correlating audio valence with the actual sentiment of the lyrics.
  2. Predictive Hit Modeling: Building a classifier to predict whether a song will become a "Top 10" hit based on its audio features.
  3. Artist Longevity: Analyzing if artists who maintain a consistent "sonic signature" last longer than those who frequently switch styles.
